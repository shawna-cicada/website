# Cicada Agility Website Redesign Brief

## Project Goal

Redesign and rebuild **cicadaagility.com** as a modern, cohesive, high-conversion consulting website that positions Cicada Agility as the partner for growing companies whose leadership, operations, and ways of working must evolve.

The new site should preserve:

- The current tagline: **Growth Happens in Stages. Leadership Must Evolve With It.**
- The cicada metaphor of transformation, emergence, renewal, and growth.
- The **Shed → Emerge → Expand** framework.
- The strongest existing service concepts.
- The credibility and complementary experience of co-founders Shawna Cullinan and Julia Kaissling.

The new site should improve:

- The clarity of what Cicada Agility helps companies solve.
- The pathway from first visit to meaningful action.
- The visual cohesion and perceived sophistication of the brand.
- The presentation of assessments, services, insights, and client credibility.
- The ability to publish content without changing code.
- The ability to book a conversation directly from the site.

---

## Primary Audience

### Primary buyers

- Founders and CEOs of growing companies.
- Executive leadership teams navigating a new stage of growth.
- Product, operations, people, and transformation leaders.
- Companies moving from founder-led execution toward scalable leadership and operating systems.
- Leadership teams experiencing friction, stalled decisions, weak accountability, unclear ownership, or delivery challenges.

### What they may be experiencing

- The company has grown, but the way it operates has not.
- Decisions take longer and involve too many people.
- Leaders believe they are aligned but act from different assumptions.
- The founder is still the center of too many decisions.
- Teams are busy, but important outcomes are not moving.
- Roles and accountability are unclear.
- Culture feels strained as the organization changes.
- Product, operations, people, and strategy are working as separate systems.
- AI adoption is happening inconsistently and without shared norms.
- The organization needs to change, but leaders cannot agree on where to begin.

## Booking and Calendar Integration

### Recommended approach

Create a dedicated `/book` page with an embedded scheduling tool.

Preferred options:

1. **Calendly**

   - Familiar scheduling experience.
   - Supports routing forms and multiple event types.

   - Greater control and customization.

### Requirements

- Embed the calendar without forcing users away from the site.
- Provide event types for:
  - Discovery Call
  - Assessment Debrief
  - Existing Client Session

- Pass source and campaign parameters when supported.
- Track calendar views, booking starts, and successful bookings.
- Provide a fallback link if the embed is blocked.
- Make booking accessible on mobile.
- Do not expose private calendar details.
- Display the correct time zone automatically.
- Include consent and privacy copy where needed.

### LinkedIn publishing

The site should create the article first and use its public URL as the canonical source.

Provide a CMS field that generates or stores suggested LinkedIn copy.

Do not assume direct automatic posting to LinkedIn will work without an approved LinkedIn application and the correct organization permissions.

Support this implementation order:

#### Phase 1
- Publish the article or video on Cicada Agility.
- Generate suggested LinkedIn post copy.
- Add a “Copy LinkedIn Post” action in the CMS or admin workflow.
- Include the canonical article URL.
- Post manually to the Cicada Agility LinkedIn company page.

#### Phase 2
- Connect the CMS webhook to Zapier, Make, or a small serverless function.
- Create a draft or scheduled social post.
- Require human approval before posting.

#### Phase 3
- Direct LinkedIn API publishing only after the organization has the required app access and permissions.

The website build should isolate social syndication behind an interface so the provider can change later.

## Visual Design Direction

### Brand qualities

- Evolving
- Intelligent
- Human
- Grounded
- Bold without being loud
- Sophisticated but not corporate
- Organic without becoming whimsical
- Strategic without becoming sterile

### Avoid

- Generic stock photos of meetings and sticky notes.
- Literal insect imagery throughout the site.
- Overuse of gradients.
- Excessive rounded cards.
- A generic SaaS-template appearance.
- Agile jargon as the primary brand language.
- Animation that delays access to content.
- Decorative motion with no relationship to the story.

### Visual language

Use a design system inspired by:

- Cicada wings and translucent layering.
- Molting and emergence.
- Sound waves and rhythm.
- Growth rings and stages.
- Natural structures paired with precise editorial typography.
- Large areas of negative space.
- Strong typographic hierarchy.
- Rich editorial photography.
- Simple custom line illustrations and icons.

### Color direction

Retain recognizable Cicada brand equity while modernizing the palette.

Suggested structure:

- Deep ink or near-black for authority.
- Warm ivory or soft mineral background.
- One distinctive cicada green or chartreuse accent.
- A secondary amber, rust, or copper tone suggesting emergence.
- Muted stone and sage supporting colors.

All final color combinations must meet WCAG AA contrast requirements.

### Typography

Use two complementary typefaces:

- Expressive serif or editorial display face for major headings.
- Highly readable sans serif for body text, navigation, and UI.

Use variable fonts where practical and self-host only with appropriate licensing.

### Animation

Use animation selectively:

- Slow hero reveal.
- Scroll-linked Shed → Emerge → Expand transformation.
- Subtle card elevation or visual shift on hover.
- Client logo marquee.
- Editorial image masks or layered wing-inspired movement.
- Staggered section entrances.
- Animated underline or directional arrow for calls to action.

Requirements:

- Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`.
- Avoid large layout shifts.
- Keep interactions smooth on mid-range mobile devices.
- Use CSS animation where possible.
- Use Framer Motion only when it adds meaningful control.

## Admin and Editorial Experience

The administrative experience is a core product requirement, not an implementation detail.

Cicada Agility’s content editor may be nontechnical. Publishing an article or video must feel more like completing a guided form than managing a traditional CMS.

### Primary editorial goal

A nontechnical user should be able to create, preview, schedule, publish, update, or unpublish an article without developer support and without understanding components, schemas, slugs, metadata, or code.

### Recommended approach

Use a customized CMS studio rather than exposing the default CMS configuration without modification.

The admin experience should:

- Use plain-language labels.
- Hide technical fields unless they are needed.
- Present fields in the order an editor naturally thinks.
- Provide clear instructions directly beside each field.
- Use visual previews.
- Prevent incomplete or broken articles from being published.
- Automatically generate technical values such as slugs, reading time, canonical URLs, and structured data.
- Offer templates for common content types.
- Provide a clear Draft → Review → Scheduled → Published workflow.
- Make it obvious what is live on the website.

### Admin dashboard

Create a branded Cicada Agility admin landing page with large, clear actions:

- **Create a New Article**
- **Add a Video**
- **Edit Existing Content**
- **Schedule a Post**
- **Update Homepage Content**
- **Manage Assessments**
- **Manage Client Logos**
- **Preview the Website**

The dashboard should also show:

- Recent drafts.
- Scheduled posts.
- Recently published articles.
- Content missing required information.
- Quick links to the live article.
- A clear publishing status badge.
- The last editor and last updated date.

Avoid opening the editor into a generic list of technical content types.

### Guided article workflow

The “Create a New Article” experience should be a simple, step-by-step workflow.

#### Step 1: Choose the content type

Present visual choices:

- Article
- Video
- Conversation or podcast
- Guide
- Case insight

Do not ask the editor to understand a content schema.

#### Step 2: Add the main content

Show only the essential fields first:

- Title
- Short summary
- Author
- Main image
- Article body or video link
- Category

Use a clean writing canvas with:

- Headings
- Paragraphs
- Bulleted and numbered lists
- Pull quotes
- Links
- Images
- Embedded video
- Callout boxes

Avoid exposing raw Markdown, HTML, JSON, or portable-text data.

#### Step 3: Review how it appears

Provide a true website preview for desktop and mobile.

The preview should show:

- Headline
- Author
- Publication date
- Main image
- Body content
- Related articles
- Social sharing image
- CTA placement

Allow the editor to move directly between editing and previewing.

#### Step 4: Prepare promotion

Automatically create suggested LinkedIn post copy from:

- Article title
- Summary
- Key takeaway
- Canonical article URL

Allow the editor to:

- Edit the suggested copy.
- Copy it with one click.
- Mark the LinkedIn post as ready or posted.
- Save the final LinkedIn post URL.

#### Step 5: Publish

Offer three clear actions:

- Save Draft
- Schedule
- Publish Now

Before publishing, show a plain-language checklist:

- Title is complete.
- Summary is complete.
- Main image is present.
- Image has alternative text.
- Author is selected.
- Category is selected.
- Article has been previewed.
- Search and social preview are ready.

Do not allow publication when critical fields are missing.

### Article templates

Provide reusable article templates so an editor does not begin with an empty page.

Suggested templates:

#### Leadership Insight
- The situation
- What leaders often assume
- What is actually happening
- What to try
- Closing reflection

#### Founder Lesson
- The growth-stage challenge
- Why the old approach stops working
- What must evolve
- Questions for founders

#### Practical Guide
- The problem
- What good looks like
- Steps to take
- Common mistakes
- Download or next step

#### Video Post
- Video
- Short introduction
- Key takeaways
- Transcript or summary
- Related resource

#### Client or Case Insight
- Context
- Challenge
- Approach
- What changed
- Lessons for other leaders

Templates should provide prompts and example structure without publishing placeholder copy.

### Plain-language field labels

Use labels such as:

- “What is the title?”
- “How would you summarize this?”
- “Who wrote this?”
- “What image should appear at the top?”
- “What should people learn from this?”
- “When should this be published?”
- “What should appear when this is shared?”

Avoid labels such as:

- Document
- Schema type
- Portable text
- Canonical
- Open Graph
- Slug
- Reference
- Taxonomy

Technical fields may still exist, but should be generated automatically or placed in an optional “Advanced settings” section.

### Automatic behavior

The admin should automatically:

- Generate the page URL from the title.
- Warn about duplicate URLs.
- Calculate reading time.
- Resize and optimize uploaded images.
- Create default search metadata from the title and summary.
- Create a default social sharing image.
- Add author and publication information.
- Recommend related articles based on category.
- Save drafts automatically.
- Warn before leaving with unsaved changes.
- Preserve article revisions.
- Allow a prior version to be restored.
- Validate external video links.
- Display image dimensions and cropping previews.
- Show how the article will appear on LinkedIn and in search results.

### Permissions

Support simple roles:

#### Editor
- Create and edit drafts.
- Upload images.
- Preview content.
- Prepare LinkedIn copy.
- Cannot change site-wide configuration.

#### Publisher
- All Editor abilities.
- Publish, schedule, unpublish, and restore content.

#### Administrator
- Manage users.
- Manage site settings.
- Manage services, assessments, navigation, and integrations.

Do not give every user full administrative access.

### Content safety and guardrails

- Confirm before unpublishing or deleting content.
- Prefer archiving over permanent deletion.
- Prevent accidental navigation changes.
- Prevent deletion of referenced authors or categories.
- Display clear validation messages in everyday language.
- Include visible help text and examples.
- Add a “Need help?” area linking to a short editorial guide.
- Maintain content history and identify who made each change.

### Editorial acceptance criteria

The admin experience is complete when a first-time, nontechnical editor can:

1. Sign in.
2. Select “Create a New Article.”
3. Choose a template.
4. Write or paste content.
5. Add an image.
6. Preview the article on desktop and mobile.
7. Generate and copy LinkedIn text.
8. Save, schedule, or publish.
9. Find and edit the article later.
10. Complete the workflow without developer guidance.

Conduct usability testing with at least one nontechnical person before launch. Observe the user rather than explaining the workflow. Any step that requires verbal explanation should be simplified.

## Definition of Done

The first production release is complete when:

- The site is responsive and accessible.
- The homepage clearly explains who Cicada helps, what changes, and how to begin.
- The primary assessment and booking pathways work.
- Services, assessments, insights, founders, and client logos are CMS-managed.
- Existing high-value URLs are migrated or redirected.
- The calendar embed works across desktop and mobile.
- Analytics record primary conversion events.
- The LinkedIn workflow generates ready-to-use post copy and article URLs.
- Editors can publish an article or video without developer support.
- The admin opens to a branded, plain-language dashboard rather than a generic CMS document list.
- A nontechnical editor can complete the article workflow without being taught CMS terminology.
- Reduced motion, keyboard navigation, SEO metadata, and structured data are implemented.
- Forms have spam protection and clear success/error states.
- Production deployment, domain configuration, and rollback steps are documented.
